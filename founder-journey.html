From Enterprise Executive to Marketplace Founder

Why Build a Marketplace from Scratch

In August 2023, I made a deliberate decision to leave the security of executive roles at established enterprises and build a marketplace from zero. The question wasn't whether I could—seventeen years at Coles managing a $1.1B P&L, and prior experience scaling Catch and Lasoo had equipped me with the operational playbook. The real question was: what was worth building that didn't already exist?

The answer lay at the intersection of three forces: a significant gap in circular fashion for South Asian markets, the sustainability imperative reshaping consumer behavior, and the untapped potential of community commerce models.

The Market Gap

While platforms like Poshmark and Depop have scaled in North America and the UK, the South Asian diaspora—culturally rooted in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh—lacked a dedicated marketplace for secondhand fashion that reflected their aesthetic, brand preferences, and values around sustainability. This wasn't a minor omission. The South Asian diaspora in the United States, Canada, and the UK represents billions in purchasing power, yet they were forced to navigate English-first platforms built for American fast fashion resellers.

The Sustainability Thesis

The fast fashion industry produces 92 million tons of textile waste annually. The environmental cost is staggering, but the market response to sustainable fashion has been bifurcated: luxury resale platforms cater to high-net-worth individuals, while budget-conscious consumers are underserved. Circular fashion isn't a niche; it's a necessity. Building a marketplace that made secondhand South Asian fashion accessible, beautiful, and community-driven felt like the right problem to solve.

Community Commerce as the Model

I had watched traditional marketplaces optimize for transaction volume. But the most resilient platforms—from eBay's collector communities to Etsy's artisan networks—succeed by building trust and identity around shared values. For PurvX, community commerce meant creating a space where South Asian fashion sellers and buyers could connect authentically, celebrate heritage, and support sustainable consumption together. This model required more than a platform; it required intention in design, culture, and seller experience.

The PurvX Story

PurvX launched as both a website (PurvX.com) and an India-focused marketplace (PurvX.in) in August 2023. "Purv" means "east" in Sanskrit—a deliberate naming choice to anchor the brand in South Asian heritage while looking forward.

Building Operations from Zero

My first days as founder were spent in unglamorous operational work: writing seller onboarding documentation, configuring payment processors, and designing the first dashboards. I was based simultaneously in San Francisco and Chennai, making the dual-geography operational challenge a feature rather than a bug. San Francisco provided access to venture capital networks and tech talent; Chennai provided deep market knowledge, supplier relationships, and cultural authenticity.

The early architecture decisions mattered. Rather than building a custom marketplace platform from scratch—a multi-quarter engineering effort—I made the pragmatic choice to use configurable marketplace software as a foundation, then layer custom workflows on top. This meant we could focus engineering effort on the problems unique to circular fashion: authentication of designer items, condition assessment workflows, and cross-border shipping logistics.

End-to-End Product Development

I owned the product roadmap directly. Key features in the first six months included:

  • Seller Onboarding Funnel: A multi-step process that qualified sellers, collected authentication documentation, and established trust before they could list. This wasn't just about compliance; it was about signaling quality to buyers from day one.
  • Seller Dashboard: Providing sellers with inventory management, analytics, and earnings tracking. Transparency about how many views their listings received, conversion rates, and seasonal trends empowered them to optimize their own selling strategies.
  • Automated Workflows: From listing reminders to payment disbursement, automation reduced manual touchpoints and made the seller experience feel modern and professional.
  • Buyer Acquisition Tools: Search filters optimized for how South Asian fashion consumers shop (by region of origin, occasion, designer legacy), and curated collections celebrating heritage and sustainability.
  • Payments and Cross-Border Logistics: Integrating with payment processors that understood South Asian card networks and negotiating shipping partnerships for the US-India route, which was underserved compared to US-UK.

Growth in the First Six Months

By early 2024, PurvX had grown the user base 800% in six months. This wasn't through large paid acquisition campaigns; it was through disciplined execution of organic channels: SEO-led discovery, early community building on social media (particularly Instagram and WhatsApp communities), and word-of-mouth from early sellers who became advocates.

Zero-to-One: Building Without External Funding

PurvX is bootstrapped. No venture capital. No debt financing. This constraint shaped every decision.

Bootstrapping Discipline

Bootstrapping forces clarity. When you're not raising money, you can't afford to dilute your focus or build for an imagined future market. Every feature, every hiring decision, every tool had to earn its keep. I reinvested early transaction revenue directly into product and growth, creating a cycle where improving the platform led to more transactions, which funded further improvements.

The resource allocation followed the Pareto principle ruthlessly. Rather than hiring a full operations team, I identified the highest-leverage functions—seller enablement and payment reconciliation—and addressed those first. Rather than building custom UI components, I used design systems and frameworks efficiently. Rather than maintaining expensive office space, I worked distributed, focusing resources on the product and people closest to the market.

SEO-Led Organic Growth

Paid user acquisition is expensive, especially for a circular fashion marketplace where customer lifetime value takes time to mature. Instead, I invested in SEO from day one. This meant:

  • Building a content strategy around long-tail keywords: "buy secondhand sarees online," "vintage Anita Dongre designs," "sustainable South Asian fashion."
  • Optimizing product pages as indexable content, so each listing contributed to organic discovery.
  • Creating authoritative content on circular fashion, heritage fashion, and sustainable consumption—not just product listings.
  • Building internal linking architecture that connected products, collections, and educational content, improving crawlability and relevance signals.

The compounding effect of SEO meant that as the platform grew, organic traffic accelerated. By month six, over 60% of new user signups came from organic search, with essentially zero customer acquisition cost.

Positioning Against Poshmark and Depop

Rather than compete head-to-head with Poshmark and Depop on their terms, PurvX carved a distinct niche. Poshmark and Depop optimize for breadth and social dynamics; PurvX optimized for cultural specificity and authentication quality. Marketing positioned PurvX not as a fashion resale platform, but as "the marketplace for South Asian heritage fashion"—a differentiation that made the bootstrap strategy viable. We weren't trying to be everything to everyone; we were essential to a specific community.

Product & Growth Lessons

Every founder learns by building. These are the specific lessons from scaling PurvX in the early stages.

Seller Conversion Optimization: The 3.2x Improvement

In the first iteration, our seller onboarding funnel lost 70% of applicants between initial inquiry and first listing approval. This was a bottleneck that limited supply growth.

Rather than blame sellers for being uncommitted, I analyzed the funnel step-by-step. The friction points emerged:

  • Documentation collection was unclear. Sellers didn't understand what "condition assessment photos" meant or how to provide them.
  • Approval latency was too high. Some sellers waited five days for feedback, then abandoned the process.
  • The value proposition wasn't communicated before they invested effort. Sellers didn't know what commission rate to expect or how often they could expect payouts.

We rebuilt the funnel with three changes: (1) Video guidance showing exactly what documentation was needed and why. (2) Automated approval for sellers with verified credentials, reducing human review latency. (3) A transparent pricing and payout schedule presented upfront. The result was a 3.2x improvement in seller conversion, with qualified sellers moving from application to first listing in 48 hours instead of two weeks.

Onboarding Funnel Design as Core Competency

Traditional ecommerce focuses on buyer acquisition funnels. Marketplaces must optimize both sides. The seller onboarding funnel is where trust is established, expectations are set, and the quality of the marketplace is determined. Every question I encountered from sellers—"How do I price my items?" "What if a buyer disputes the quality?"—became an opportunity to design a better experience and reduce future support burden.

Community Commerce Dynamics

Building community isn't a marketing tactic; it's a product feature. PurvX invested in community-building mechanisms from the start: curated collections featuring seller stories, community guidelines that established behavioral norms, and social features that highlighted active sellers and celebrated standout items. The marketplace became a space where sellers felt seen, not just like a channel to move inventory.

Cross-Border Operations: The San Francisco-Chennai Model

Operating from two geographies created complexity but also competitive advantage. San Francisco provided access to tech talent, venture capital networks, and understanding of North American market dynamics. Chennai provided supplier relationships, cultural fluency, and operational cost efficiency. The lesson was: don't try to be globally distributed for the sake of it, but if you're solving a problem that spans geographies, make that a feature. For PurvX, the dual-geography model meant we could source inventory in India, authenticate items locally, and ship to diaspora customers in North America with efficiency that a single-geography operation couldn't match.

Enterprise Experience as Founder Advantage

One of the most valuable insights from this journey: the skills that made me effective managing $1.1B in retail P&L at Coles translate directly to founding a marketplace. Not because the scale is the same—it's not—but because the fundamental problems are similar.

Operations at Any Scale

At Coles, I managed hundreds of stores, thousands of SKUs, and millions of transactions annually. The core operational discipline was identical: define the process, measure the outputs, identify bottlenecks, iterate. At PurvX, the same discipline applies to the seller onboarding funnel, buyer acquisition, and payment reconciliation. Scale changes the specifics, but the approach is timeless.

Supplier Relationship Management

In a $1.1B retail business, supplier relationships are critical. At Coles, this meant negotiating with manufacturers for favorable terms, managing inventory with precision, and ensuring consistency across locations. At PurvX, sellers are our suppliers. The lesson translated: treat them as partners, not just transaction counterparties. Provide transparency, offer tools to succeed, and create incentives aligned with your marketplace's success. This is why the seller dashboard and analytics were prioritized—they enable sellers to optimize, which improves supply quality and encourages retention.

Data-Driven Decision-Making

At Coles, every inventory decision was informed by POS data, supplier performance metrics, and customer feedback. At PurvX, the same discipline applies: conversion funnels, seller cohort retention, buyer search behavior. The enterprise experience instilled a habit of asking "what does the data say?" before making intuitive leaps. This prevented us from building features we thought were good and instead focused us on evidence of actual user needs.

Leveraging Prior Marketplace Work

My experience scaling Lasoo from catalogue to marketplace and building supply-side operations at Catch was invaluable. I had seen which marketplace models work and which become unsustainable. I understood the dual-sided problem, the importance of seller incentives, and the non-linearity of marketplace growth. I also understood what doesn't scale: manual processes, inconsistent seller support, and unclear trust mechanisms. PurvX was designed to avoid those pitfalls from day one.

Data Standards and Interoperability

At GS1, I worked on product data standards and partnerships with Amazon, Google, and Alibaba. This experience informed how PurvX structures product metadata, making it indexable by search engines and compatible with future integrations. It's easy to overlook when you're building a small marketplace, but getting data architecture right from the start prevents painful refactors later.

What Comes Next

PurvX is still early, but the trajectory is clear. Three areas shape the forward vision.

Circular Commerce Expansion

Fashion is the entry point, but circular commerce principles apply across categories: home decor, textiles, jewelry, accessories. The South Asian market has unique design heritage in all these categories—from hand-loomed sarees to traditionally crafted jewelry—that deserves its own marketplace. The buildout from fashion to adjacent categories will happen as the core platform matures and sellers request new listing categories.

South Asian Diaspora as Anchor Market

The diaspora in the United States, Canada, and the UK represents the initial focus. This population is concentrated, culturally connected (through family ties, social networks, religious communities), and underserved. Once we've achieved deep penetration in diaspora markets, the logical expansion is back to source: making PurvX the go-to secondhand marketplace in South Asia itself, serving middle-class consumers seeking sustainable fashion at scale.

Cross-Border Marketplace Potential

The long-term vision is to make cross-border transactions between South Asia and diaspora markets as frictionless as domestic transactions. This requires solving complex problems in logistics, payments, customs, and trust. But it's the opportunity that excites me most: enabling an Indian seller to reach customers in San Francisco as easily as reaching customers in Delhi. That infrastructure doesn't exist yet at scale, but PurvX's dual-geography model positions us to build it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a circular marketplace?

A circular marketplace is a platform that facilitates buying and selling of secondhand, pre-owned, or vintage items with the goal of extending product lifecycles, reducing waste, and promoting sustainable consumption. In fashion, circular marketplaces like PurvX enable consumers to buy and sell quality used clothing, reducing environmental footprint and providing access to heritage and designer pieces at affordable prices.

How do you build a marketplace from scratch?

Building a marketplace requires solving the dual-sided problem: acquiring sellers and buyers simultaneously. Key steps include identifying a niche gap, building foundational technology (seller onboarding, payment systems, buyer acquisition), designing seller workflows and dashboards, optimizing the onboarding funnel, leveraging organic growth channels, and iterating based on community feedback. Success depends on getting early traction on both supply and demand sides, which is why clarity on who you're serving and why matters before you build.

What is community commerce?

Community commerce emphasizes building trusted networks of buyers and sellers around shared values—such as sustainability, cultural identity, or niche interests. Rather than treating the marketplace as purely transactional, community commerce fosters relationships, enables curation by community members, and creates a sense of belonging. For South Asian fashion, community commerce means celebrating diaspora heritage while promoting sustainable consumption and supporting artisans and resellers within the community.

How do you grow a marketplace organically?

Organic growth strategies for marketplaces include SEO-led acquisition targeting long-tail keywords related to your niche, content marketing that educates buyers and sellers, referral programs that incentivize word-of-mouth, community engagement in social channels, and continuous optimization of the onboarding funnel. The goal is to make user acquisition cost-efficient and sustainable. PurvX achieved 800% user growth in six months primarily through organic channels—over 60% of new signups came from search with essentially zero customer acquisition cost.

What is the difference between building and buying marketplace technology?

Building your own marketplace technology from the ground up allows deep customization of the user experience, seller workflows, and growth mechanisms, but requires significant engineering resources and time. Buying or adopting off-the-shelf platforms (like Mirakl) accelerates time-to-market but reduces flexibility. The choice depends on your resources, timeline, and need for differentiation. For PurvX, the pragmatic approach was to use configurable marketplace software as a foundation, then customize and layer unique workflows on top—particularly around authentication, condition assessment, and South Asian market dynamics.

What Merchandising Means in a Marketplace

Marketplace merchandising is fundamentally different from traditional retail merchandising. While retail merchandising focuses on optimizing owned inventory and physical shelf space to maximize sales per square foot, marketplace merchandising operates in a multi-seller, multi-category ecosystem where the marketplace operator doesn't own the inventory but manages the visibility, positioning, and discoverability of third-party seller products.

In a marketplace context, Sengupta's role as Head of Merchandising at Catch involved orchestrating how tens of thousands of products from hundreds of sellers were presented to millions of customers. This requires a different skill set than managing a single retail brand. The merchandiser becomes a conductor of supply and demand, balancing category strategy, seller performance, pricing dynamics, and customer experience simultaneously.

Key differences between retail and marketplace merchandising include:

  • Ownership Structure: Retail is about owned inventory; marketplaces are about curated third-party inventory
  • Pricing Control: Retailers set prices; marketplace merchandisers influence pricing through promotions and seller incentives
  • Data Dependencies: Retail depends on internal systems; marketplaces integrate data from multiple sellers and platforms
  • Category Breadth: Retailers manage focused categories; marketplaces often span hundreds of categories across different seller capabilities
  • Promotion Complexity: Retail promotions are straightforward; marketplace promotions require seller participation and alignment

Understanding this distinction is crucial because it shapes how you approach every merchandising decision. You're not optimizing for one retailer's margins—you're optimizing for an entire ecosystem's health while maintaining platform profitability.

Key Takeaway: Marketplace merchandising requires managing a multi-seller ecosystem where your role is to orchestrate supply, demand, and visibility across hundreds of categories—not simply optimizing owned inventory like traditional retail.

Managing 800+ Digital Storefronts

At Coles, Sengupta managed merchandising strategy across 800+ digital storefronts with a $1.1B P&L. This scale represents one of Australia's largest ecommerce operations and presents unique complexity that most retailers never face.

Managing 800+ storefronts is not simply 800 times the complexity of managing one storefront. The challenges multiply exponentially:

Regional Variation and Local Relevance

Each digital storefront serves a specific geographic region or store catchment. A metropolitan Coles store in Melbourne has dramatically different customer demographics, purchasing patterns, and inventory availability than a suburban Coles in Brisbane. Sengupta's merchandising strategy had to account for these regional variations while maintaining brand consistency and operational efficiency.

This required sophisticated segmentation of the product catalog, promotional calendar, and category focus. A storefront in a high-income area might emphasize premium organic products, while another storefront serves price-sensitive customers. Rather than creating entirely unique assortments for each location, Sengupta's approach involved creating a base national assortment with localization layers that could be applied based on store characteristics.

Inventory Availability and Fulfillment Constraints

Managing 800 storefronts means managing 800 different inventory positions. A product might be in stock at some locations but not others. Your merchandising strategy must account for this variability. Showing a customer a product they can't actually access locally creates friction and damages trust.

This led to the development of intelligent inventory display rules that showed products based on actual stock availability at the customer's nearest fulfillment point. Rather than a simple "in stock/out of stock" flag, Sengupta's team implemented sophisticated inventory visibility rules that balanced product promotion with fulfillment reality.

Category Management Across Store Formats

Coles operates different store formats—supermarkets, hypermarkets, and convenience stores—each with different product assortments and customer expectations. A convenience store customer has different needs than a hypermarket customer. Your merchandising approach must recognize these store format differences.

Sengupta's category management approach involved creating different category hierarchies, product hierarchies, and merchandising rules for different store formats. This prevented customers at convenience stores from being overwhelmed with 500+ SKUs in a category when only 30 might be available locally.

Operational Feasibility and Change Management

Making a single change to one storefront is simple. Making a coordinated change across 800 storefronts while managing different local requirements, inventory positions, and business rules is extraordinarily complex. This is where merchandising operations become as important as merchandising strategy.

Sengupta's team developed templated merchandising approaches where changes could be applied systematically while still allowing local customization. Rather than manually configuring each storefront, they built rule-based systems that could generate appropriate merchandising configurations based on store characteristics, category performance, and inventory availability.

Gross Margin Optimisation

At Catch, Sengupta maintained gross margins above 40% on a $200M P&L while managing a highly competitive marketplace environment. In Australia's ecommerce landscape, where price competition is intense and consumer price sensitivity is high, maintaining premium margins requires a sophisticated multi-part strategy.

Premium Seller Recruitment and Curation

Gross margin optimization starts before merchandising—it starts with seller selection. Rather than accepting all sellers, Sengupta's approach at Catch involved selective recruitment of sellers who could deliver quality products at attractive unit economics. This meant focusing on established brands and sellers with premium positioning rather than chasing volume with low-margin sellers.

This approach seems counterintuitive—fewer sellers and less selection should theoretically hurt platform growth. In practice, however, it creates a virtuous cycle: premium sellers attract premium customers, premium customers have lower price sensitivity, lower price sensitivity supports higher margins, and higher margins fund better marketing and platform investment.

Strategic Promotional Cadence

The biggest margin killer in ecommerce is uncontrolled promotional intensity. When every seller is running promotions constantly, customers learn to never buy full price, and margins collapse. Sengupta's approach involved managing promotional intensity—not eliminating promotions, but orchestrating them strategically.

This involved working with sellers to establish promotional windows and intensity targets. Rather than allowing the race-to-bottom dynamic where sellers compete on discount depth, Sengupta's team created a promotional calendar that distributed promotional activity across the year while maintaining brand integrity. Seasonal peaks like back-to-school and Christmas received heavy promotional investment, while other periods maintained more stable pricing.

Dynamic Pricing and Competitive Intelligence

Maintaining 40%+ margins in a competitive marketplace requires understanding competitive dynamics. Sengupta's team implemented competitive pricing intelligence that tracked competitor offerings while maintaining pricing discipline. Rather than reflexively matching competitor price cuts, they analyzed whether the competitive threat was material to customer acquisition or primarily affecting margin.

Dynamic pricing systems allowed for automated adjustments to maintain margin targets while responding to market conditions. This wasn't about race-to-bottom pricing; it was about intelligent pricing that maintained brand positioning while remaining competitive.

Category Margin Architecture

Not all categories can sustain 40% margins. Electronics typically have 10-15% margins; jewelry can support 50%+ margins. Sengupta's approach involved architecting the category portfolio to achieve the overall margin target while acknowledging category-specific realities. This meant being aggressive in high-margin categories like home décor and selective in low-margin necessities like groceries or batteries.

The merchandiser's role is to understand which categories drive traffic (even at lower margins) and which categories drive profit. This understanding informs merchandising positioning, investment level, and promotional intensity.

Inventory and Days on Hand

Days on Hand (DOH) is a critical metric in marketplace and retail operations. It measures how long inventory sits before being sold. At Catch, Sengupta maintained DOH under 45 days—an exceptional achievement in an Australian marketplace context where inventory typically turns more slowly than in North America.

Keeping DOH low is critical because inventory carrying costs are substantial. Every dollar of inventory not sold is capital tied up, warehousing costs incurred, and risk of obsolescence or markdown exposure. For a $200M marketplace operation, the difference between 45-day and 60-day DOH represents millions in annual carrying cost savings.

Demand Planning and Forecasting

The foundation of DOH management is accurate demand planning. Sengupta's team used historical sales data, seasonal trends, and forward indicators to forecast demand at the SKU level. In a marketplace, this is complicated because you're forecasting across thousands of SKUs with varying velocity, seasonality, and predictability.

Rather than attempting to forecast every SKU with precision, Sengupta's approach involved clustering SKUs by characteristics and applying appropriate forecasting methodologies to each cluster. Fast-movers used recent sales velocity; slow-moving seasonal items used historical seasonal patterns; new items used analogous product performance.

Strategic Promotional Timing

Promotional strategy directly impacts DOH. The primary purpose of promotions isn't just to drive incremental sales—it's also to clear inventory before it ages and becomes a carrying cost problem. Sengupta's team tracked aging inventory and strategically promoted products approaching slow inventory thresholds.

This required coordination with sellers. Rather than forcing clearance promotions that damage margins excessively, Sengupta worked with sellers to front-load inventory into promotions before products aged significantly. This approach maintained reasonable margins while keeping inventory turning.

Dynamic Pricing Strategies

Inventory health should directly impact pricing. Products with high inventory relative to sales velocity should receive less aggressive pricing; low-inventory items can command premium positioning. Sengupta's approach involved building inventory-health metrics into the pricing framework.

When a product had 90 days of inventory at current velocity, dynamic pricing systems would reduce its visibility or apply pricing pressure. When inventory fell to 10 days of stock, pricing support increased and merchandising emphasized the product. This created a natural inventory equilibration mechanism.

Seller Education and Accountability

In a marketplace, the merchandise operator controls visibility and merchandising but sellers control inventory investment decisions. Sengupta's approach involved educating sellers about their own DOH metrics and creating accountability for overstock situations.

Sellers with consistently poor DOH received less favorable merchandising treatment. This created an incentive system where sellers who managed inventory well received better visibility and higher sales velocity, creating a self-reinforcing positive cycle. Poor inventory managers naturally faced margin pressure as their products received lower priority placement.

Automating Merchandising Operations

Managing 800+ storefronts with a reasonable team size is only possible through automation. At Coles, Sengupta worked with IT and data teams to digitize manual merchandising workflows and reduce operational overhead. This transformation from manual merchandising configuration to data-driven automation was fundamental to operating at scale.

The Manual Merchandising Problem

In traditional retail operations, merchandisers manually configure store planograms, decide which products to display, create promotional calendars, and manage category hierarchies. This works at small scale but becomes operationally infeasible at 800 locations.

For example, manually creating promotional calendars for 800 storefronts, each with different inventory positions, store formats, and local market conditions, would require hundreds of merchandisers. Instead, Sengupta's team developed automated systems that could generate appropriate promotional calendars based on store characteristics, historical performance, and inventory availability.

Rule-Based Merchandising Engines

Sengupta's approach involved building rule-based systems that could apply consistent merchandising principles across the entire operation. Rather than static configurations, these systems used real-time data to make merchandising decisions.

For example, a rule-based system might specify: "Promote products with inventory over 60 days at 15% discount; products over 90 days at 25% discount; feature best-selling SKUs in category landing pages; rotate featured products weekly based on sales velocity; apply location-specific product filtering based on store format." These rules, when automated, could generate appropriate merchandising configurations for all 800 storefronts without manual intervention.

Data Integration and Single Source of Truth

Automation requires consistent data. Sengupta's team built data pipelines that integrated inventory systems, sales systems, product catalogs, and competitor pricing data into a single source of truth. Without this data integration, rules-based merchandising becomes impossible.

This involved working closely with IT to understand data availability, quality, and freshness. Inventory data needed to be updated frequently (ideally daily); sales data needed to be available at the SKU and location level; product data needed to be complete and accurate.

A/B Testing and Continuous Optimization

With thousands of daily merchandising decisions happening automatically, the opportunity for continuous optimization emerges. Sengupta's approach involved implementing A/B testing frameworks that could test different merchandising approaches and measure impact.

For example, testing whether category page layouts favoring "best sellers" outperformed layouts favoring "trending products" could be tested across multiple storefronts and measured against conversion metrics. Winning variations could then be rolled out systematically across the operation.

Exception Handling and Manual Override

While automation is powerful, edge cases and exceptional situations always emerge. Sengupta's systems included exception handling mechanisms where human merchandisers could intervene in specific situations. A new product launch might require manual merchandising configuration; a competitive threat might warrant manual intervention; a seasonal event might require one-time promotional calendars.

The key was designing automation that handled 95% of routine cases, freeing the team to focus on exception handling and strategic initiatives rather than routine configuration work.

UX and Conversion Optimisation

Merchandising isn't only about inventory, margins, and operations—it's fundamentally about customer experience. At Catch, Sengupta improved organic traffic and conversion by 18% through enhanced site navigation and UX. This demonstrates that merchandising directly impacts conversion and business performance.

Understanding Customer Journey and Friction Points

Sengupta's approach to UX optimization started with understanding customer journeys. Where do customers enter the site? What categories do they browse? Where do they encounter friction? Where do they abandon?

Through analytics, Sengupta's team identified that many customers entering through organic search landed on category pages that were overwhelming in breadth and didn't guide them toward relevant products. The original category structures were designed for internal taxonomies, not customer mental models.

Redesigning Category Navigation

The primary fix involved restructuring category navigation to match customer mental models rather than internal taxonomies. For example, rather than organizing Fashion by "Mens | Womens | Kids" with hundreds of SKUs under each, Sengupta's team reorganized around customer occasions and contexts: "Casual Wear | Work Attire | Athletic | Occasion Wear | Accessories."

This seemingly simple change had profound impact. Customers could quickly find the specific subcategory they were seeking rather than navigating thousands of SKUs. Conversion improved because friction decreased—customers spent less time searching and more time purchasing.

Search and Discovery Optimization

Beyond category navigation, Sengupta's team optimized search and discovery. Search results were reorganized to highlight relevant attributes (size, color, brand, price) based on category. Filters were simplified to show only decision-relevant attributes rather than overwhelming customers with 50+ filter options.

Smart sorting became contextual. In categories where customers typically search by price, price was the default sort. In categories where brand matters, brand prominence increased. In fashion categories where recency matters, new arrivals received higher prominence.

Product Discovery and Personalization

Beyond navigation, Sengupta implemented personalization that helped customers discover products. "Customers who bought X also bought Y" recommendations helped cross-category discovery. "Personalized recommendations based on browsing history" helped increase basket size.

The key was ensuring that recommendations felt helpful rather than manipulative. Recommendations needed to reflect genuine product relationships, not just cross-selling algorithms optimized purely for margin.

Mobile Experience and Responsive Merchandising

The merchandising optimizations were particularly critical for mobile experience. Mobile screens have limited real estate. Showing 50 products per category page doesn't work on mobile; you might show 12. This means merchandising decisions about which 12 products to show become critical.

Sengupta's team implemented responsive merchandising where the products and categories shown varied based on device type. Bestsellers received higher prominence on mobile; detailed filters were hidden behind expandable sections to avoid overwhelming small screens.

Merchandising Across Platform Types

Sengupta has worked across different marketplace platforms—Mirakl at Catch, Marketplacer at Lasoo, and custom-built platforms at Coles. Each platform has different capabilities and constraints that shape merchandising strategy.

Mirakl-Based Marketplaces

Mirakl is a mature SaaS marketplace platform used globally. It provides built-in functionality for seller onboarding, order management, commission management, and basic merchandising. At Catch, working within the Mirakl framework meant leveraging its native capabilities while extending where necessary.

Mirakl's strength is rapid deployment and built-in compliance. Its limitation is that standard functionality may not match your specific merchandising vision. Sengupta's approach involved using Mirakl's core platform while extending merchandising capabilities through custom integrations and APIs for advanced features like dynamic pricing and AI-driven recommendations.

Marketplacer-Based Marketplaces

Marketplacer is an Australian marketplace platform with strong local expertise. At Lasoo, where Sengupta defined marketplace strategy, Marketplacer provided a platform focused on the Australian ecommerce context with built-in support for logistics, payments, and seller management tailored to the market.

The approach with Marketplacer involved aligning merchandising strategy with platform capabilities while recognizing local market dynamics. This included working with the platform vendor on roadmap priorities and feature enhancements that would support merchandising objectives.

Custom-Built Platforms

At Coles, with its scale and specific requirements, a custom platform was necessary. Custom platforms offer maximum flexibility but require significant development investment and ongoing maintenance. Sengupta's approach involved designing a platform architecture that could support the complex merchandising requirements of 800+ storefronts while remaining operationally sustainable.

Custom platforms enabled sophisticated features like location-based inventory visibility rules, format-specific category hierarchies, and complex promotional logic that would be difficult to implement on standard platforms. The trade-off was that every feature enhancement required development work.

Key Considerations in Platform Selection

When choosing between platform types, Sengupta's framework involves evaluating:

  • Scale Requirements: Do you need to support thousands of sellers and locations? Custom platforms may be necessary at extreme scale.
  • Merchandising Complexity: Do you need sophisticated dynamic pricing, personalization, or inventory rules? SaaS platforms may require significant customization.
  • Time to Market: Do you need to launch quickly? SaaS platforms allow faster go-to-market than custom development.
  • Long-term Flexibility: Will you need to evolve your merchandising strategy significantly over time? Custom platforms offer more flexibility.
  • Operational Efficiency: Can a smaller team operate and maintain the platform? SaaS platforms reduce operational burden.
Key Takeaway: Successful marketplace merchandising at scale requires automating operational decisions, maintaining promotional discipline, and optimizing customer experience. Platform selection and design must align with your specific merchandising vision and scale requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between retail merchandising and marketplace merchandising?

Retail merchandising involves managing owned inventory and physical shelf space to maximize sales per square foot. Marketplace merchandising manages third-party seller inventory across multiple platforms, focusing on category strategy, seller performance, and platform visibility. Marketplace merchandising requires coordinating with sellers, managing inventory data flows, and optimizing for both marketplace search and external traffic. The fundamental difference is ownership—retailers control inventory; marketplaces orchestrate it.

How can you maintain gross margins above 40% in a competitive marketplace?

Maintaining strong gross margins requires a three-part strategy: (1) Selective seller recruitment focused on quality brands with premium positioning rather than chasing volume, (2) Dynamic promotional cadence that avoids race-to-bottom discounting by orchestrating promotional intensity across the year, and (3) Inventory optimization that reduces carrying costs while maintaining availability. This approach, executed by Sengupta at Catch, treats margin as a system property, not an individual transaction property.

What are the key strategies for reducing Days on Hand in a marketplace?

Days on Hand reduction requires: (1) Accurate demand planning using historical data, seasonal trends, and forward indicators at the SKU level, (2) Strategic promotional timing to clear slow-moving inventory before it ages, (3) Dynamic pricing that reflects inventory health and encourages turnover, and (4) Seller education on inventory management best practices with accountability for overstock situations. At Catch, Sengupta maintained Days on Hand under 45 days through these coordinated approaches, treating inventory health as a system-level priority.

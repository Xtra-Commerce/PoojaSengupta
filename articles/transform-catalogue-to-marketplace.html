How to Transform a Digital Catalogue into a Full-Scale Marketplace

A strategic framework for evolving from a curated catalogue to a multi-seller marketplace platform, with proven tactics for seller onboarding, merchandising, and monetization.

When a Catalogue Is No Longer Enough

Digital catalogues have long served as efficient vehicles for business-to-business commerce. They provide a centralized, curated view of products, enable bulk ordering, and streamline procurement workflows. However, in an increasingly competitive digital commerce landscape, catalogues face fundamental limitations that constrain growth.

The catalogue model is inherently supply-constrained. A business controls the product assortment directly, which means inventory, product data quality, and range breadth are entirely dependent on internal operations. As customer expectations evolve and markets fragment, this constraint becomes a strategic liability. Customers demand assortment breadth, competitive pricing, and access to niche suppliers—capabilities that catalogues struggle to provide at scale.

The marketplace model addresses these constraints by distributing the supply function across multiple sellers. Instead of managing inventory directly, the platform orchestrates supplier relationships, enabling exponential growth in product range, competitive pricing dynamics, and customer choice. For businesses built on catalogue foundations, this shift represents both an operational transformation and a strategic opportunity.

The catalyst for transformation typically emerges from one of three forces: stalled growth despite market expansion, intensifying price competition from decentralized suppliers, or changing customer expectations around assortment and convenience. Recognizing these signals early is critical to executing a successful catalogue-to-marketplace transformation.

Assessing Marketplace Readiness

Not every catalogue business is ready for a marketplace transformation. The decision to pursue this path should be grounded in concrete business readiness signals rather than industry trends alone.

Foundational Requirements

Begin by evaluating whether your current infrastructure can support marketplace complexity. This includes:

  • Product data maturity: A marketplace depends on high-quality, standardized product information. If your current catalogue requires manual curation for 80% of entries, scaling to thousands of seller-managed SKUs will be operationally unfeasible.
  • Technology debt: Marketplace platforms impose new requirements: multi-seller inventory management, seller dashboards, commission tracking, and settlement workflows. Assess whether your existing technology stack can accommodate these or if rebuilding is necessary.
  • Customer base compatibility: Evaluate whether your existing customer base values the expanded assortment and competitive pricing that marketplaces provide. Some B2B segments may prefer the consistency and curation of catalogues.
  • Seller ecosystem readiness: A marketplace succeeds only if suppliers are willing to participate. Conduct preliminary outreach to key suppliers: Are they interested in selling through your channel? Do they have the systems to manage inventory and fulfillment integration?

Business Readiness Indicators

Beyond infrastructure, assess your organization's appetite for this transformation. Marketplace models introduce new commercial dynamics: supplier negotiations, dynamic pricing, competition management, and complex data governance. Does your leadership team have experience navigating these challenges?

Evaluate financial readiness as well. Building or implementing a marketplace platform requires significant investment. Calculate the total cost of ownership across platform licensing, engineering resources, seller support operations, and marketing to acquire both sellers and buyers. Ensure revenue projections justify this investment over a reasonable timeframe.

Platform Selection: Build vs Buy

One of the most consequential decisions in a catalogue-to-marketplace transformation is choosing the underlying platform. Two principal options exist: building a custom marketplace solution or adopting an established marketplace platform.

The Case for Platform Adoption

Established marketplace platforms—such as Marketplacer, Mirakl, Combify, or Shopify Plus with marketplace extensions—offer significant advantages for businesses transitioning from catalogues. These platforms were purpose-built to address the operational complexity of multi-seller commerce. They provide:

  • Accelerated time-to-market: Configuration and integration can launch a marketplace in months rather than years. For companies facing competitive pressure, this speed is often decisive.
  • Seller management infrastructure: Onboarding workflows, seller dashboards, commission management, and performance analytics are built in. Your team can focus on seller recruitment rather than building these capabilities from scratch.
  • Compliance and operational readiness: Established platforms handle VAT computation, regulatory compliance, and multi-currency settlement—complex requirements that delay custom builds.
  • Scalability without architecture risk: Platform providers manage infrastructure scaling, allowing you to focus on commercial strategy rather than engineering operations.

For Lasoo's transformation, platform adoption via Marketplacer was strategically decisive. Rather than dedicating engineering resources to building seller management infrastructure, Sengupta's team focused on the uniquely valuable work: seller recruitment, merchandising strategy, and marketplace monetization. This allocation of effort accelerated time-to-revenue and allowed the organization to respond to market feedback more quickly.

Build Considerations

Custom development makes sense primarily when your marketplace has highly specific requirements that existing platforms cannot support, or when network effects make differentiation crucial. For most catalogue-to-marketplace transitions, platform adoption is the rational choice.

Designing the Seller Acquisition Strategy

A marketplace is fundamentally a supply-and-demand coordination problem. Buyer activity depends on compelling supplier selection; supplier participation depends on demand. The inaugural seller acquisition phase is therefore critical—it sets the tone for platform credibility and determines initial assortment quality.

Tiered Seller Recruitment

Sengupta's approach at Lasoo involved a deliberate tiering strategy. The marketplace began with a cohort of large, established retailers—enterprises with existing brand recognition, operational maturity, and inventory management systems. Within six months, this strategy achieved onboarding of 60+ large retailers, a critical mass that signaled credibility to both customers and smaller sellers.

Why prioritize large retailers? Enterprise sellers bring three strategic advantages:

  • Assortment depth: A single large retailer can contribute thousands of SKUs, immediately enriching marketplace breadth without proportional operational overhead.
  • Credibility: Customers recognize established brands. Their presence on your marketplace transfers trust and legitimacy to your platform.
  • Integration precedent: Large retailers often have sophisticated systems and dedicated IT teams. Successfully integrating with them establishes technical patterns and best practices that smaller sellers can follow.

Enterprise Seller Recruitment Tactics

Recruiting 60+ enterprise sellers requires a dedicated, relationship-driven approach. Sengupta collaborated closely with product and engineering teams to design seller-specific value propositions. Key tactics included:

  • Custom integration support: Rather than requiring sellers to conform to generic APIs, offer dedicated integration engineering. This removes friction and signals commitment to their success.
  • Performance incentives: Structure initial commission rates or revenue sharing to create mutual upside. Make it financially attractive for sellers to invest in their marketplace presence.
  • Operational partnership: Beyond platform features, involve sellers in product roadmap decisions. This transforms them from vendors into stakeholders with vested interest in the marketplace's success.
  • Executive sponsorship: Enterprise negotiations often require executive engagement. Ensure your leadership team is actively involved in closing material partnerships.

The seller acquisition phase typically extends over 6-12 months and requires sustained effort from commercial, product, and engineering teams. View this as a strategic investment that pays dividends throughout the marketplace's lifecycle.

Merchandising in a Marketplace Model

The transition from catalogue to marketplace fundamentally changes merchandising strategy. In a curated catalogue, merchandising is a supply-side problem: the business selects, arranges, and optimizes product presentation. In a marketplace, merchandising becomes a platform-level responsibility that must orchestrate thousands of seller-managed product listings.

From Curation to Orchestration

Catalogue merchandising relies on human curation. A small editorial team selects products, writes descriptions, and arranges displays based on taste and market knowledge. This approach doesn't scale to marketplace sizes. Instead, marketplaces employ orchestration: they define standards, enable sellers to populate product data, and use algorithms to surface the most relevant and highest-quality listings to customers.

This shift requires investment in product data standardization. Define data schemas that capture essential product attributes—dimensions, specifications, certifications—in consistent formats. Provide sellers with clear templates and validation rules. The cleaner your data governance, the more effective your search, filtering, and recommendation algorithms become.

Quality Assurance and Seller Enablement

Sengupta's merchandising strategy at Lasoo involved close collaboration between the platform team and sellers to establish data quality standards. This included:

  • Developing comprehensive product data templates specific to key categories
  • Creating seller education resources: guides, webinars, and hands-on support for sellers learning to optimize their listings
  • Implementing automated validation and flagging mechanisms to identify substandard listings before they reach customers
  • Establishing category management functions to monitor assortment quality and identify gaps

The goal is not to return to manual curation—that's incompatible with marketplace scale—but to encode quality standards into the platform itself, enabling sellers to succeed while maintaining a consistent customer experience.

Building the Technology Stack

A successful marketplace transformation requires deliberate architecture decisions around seller integration, data management, and operational tooling.

API-First Integration Architecture

Marketplaces must accommodate sellers with varying degrees of technical sophistication. Some sellers have advanced inventory management systems and expect deep API integration; others operate with manual data entry or basic CSV imports. Design your integration architecture to support both extremes.

At Lasoo, Sengupta collaborated with engineering to establish tiered integration patterns:

  • REST APIs for sophisticated sellers: RESTful APIs enable real-time inventory synchronization, order management, and fulfillment tracking. Document comprehensively and provide sandbox environments for seller development.
  • Batch integration for less technical sellers: CSV uploads, SFTP, or EDI mechanisms allow sellers without development resources to participate. This is critical for including smaller businesses.
  • Native dashboards for manual management: Simple web interfaces for inventory updates, order management, and analytics serve sellers who operate at smaller scale.

Seller Tools and Operational Levers

Beyond integrations, invest in seller-facing tools that enable operational success. This includes:

  • Inventory management dashboards with alerts for low stock and performance insights
  • Order management systems that streamline fulfillment workflows
  • Analytics dashboards showing conversion rates, traffic sources, and revenue attribution
  • Commission and settlement reporting for financial transparency
  • Marketing support tools: promotional dashboards, campaign analytics, and audience insights

These tools serve a dual purpose: they enable sellers to operate efficiently on your platform, and they create switching costs that strengthen seller retention.

Monetisation: Beyond Commission

Traditional marketplace models rely on transaction commissions as the primary revenue stream. Sengupta's strategy at Lasoo expanded the monetization framework to unlock additional revenue streams while creating new value for sellers.

Commission Structure Design

Commission rates must balance two objectives: remaining attractive to sellers (who have alternatives) and generating meaningful revenue for the platform. Research competitor commission rates, but don't anchor to them exclusively. Consider your unique value proposition. If you provide substantial traffic, quality customer bases, or operational support, justify higher rates.

Structure commissions thoughtfully. Some marketplaces use tiered rates (lower rates for higher-volume sellers) to encourage growth and retention. Others segment by category, with higher commissions on high-margin categories and lower rates for commoditized goods. The optimal structure depends on your seller mix and market dynamics.

Retail Media as a Growth Engine

The most innovative monetization opportunity Sengupta established at Lasoo was retail media. Rather than relying solely on transaction commissions, the marketplace introduced advertising capabilities allowing sellers to bid for prominence in search results and category pages.

Retail media transformed the unit economics of the marketplace:

  • Incremental revenue stream: Sellers were willing to pay for sponsored placement, generating revenue independent of transactions.
  • Better seller economics: With advertising as an additional monetization option, sellers could justify higher transaction commissions, improving platform margins.
  • Customer experience improvement: Sponsored products created incentives for sellers to improve listing quality and competitiveness, ultimately benefiting customers through better assortment and competitive pricing.

Sengupta's retail media initiative achieved a 20% uplift in seller ad spend, demonstrating strong demand for marketplace advertising opportunities. This success underscores the importance of moving beyond transaction commissions to unlock seller monetization opportunities.

Adjacent Monetization Opportunities

Beyond commissions and advertising, explore adjacent opportunities:

  • Premium seller programs: Offer tiered membership (e.g., "Featured Seller" or "Gold Partner" badges) with benefits like prioritized support, analytics enhancements, or promotional features.
  • Data and insights services: Aggregate marketplace data to offer sellers category trends, competitive benchmarking, or demand forecasts.
  • Logistics and fulfillment services: Partner with logistics providers to offer discounted shipping or fulfillment services, earning referral fees or wholesale margins.
  • Financial services: Enable seller financing, working capital loans, or payment processing, capturing fees or interest margins.

The most successful marketplaces view monetization not as a single revenue stream but as a portfolio of complementary offerings that create value across the ecosystem.

Results and Metrics

Sengupta's transformation of Lasoo from a digital catalogue to a full-scale marketplace delivered significant commercial results that validate the strategic framework outlined above.

Seller Acquisition Results

The marketplace achieved onboarding of 60+ large retailers within the first six months. This critical mass established platform credibility and set the stage for sustained growth. The deliberate focus on enterprise sellers created high-quality assortment that differentiated the marketplace from smaller, seller-driven platforms.

Growth Metrics

The marketplace achieved 120% month-over-month GMV growth, a remarkable rate that reflects strong demand for expanded assortment, competitive pricing, and the credibility of established sellers. This growth trajectory demonstrates that customers responded positively to the marketplace model.

More importantly for sustainability, the business generated a 15% revenue uplift. While GMV growth is an important leading indicator, revenue growth is the ultimate measure of marketplace health. The 15% revenue increase reflects successful monetization across commissions and emerging revenue streams.

Retail Media Impact

The retail media initiative, one of Sengupta's strategic innovations, drove a 20% uplift in seller ad spend. This demonstrates that sellers recognized genuine value in marketplace advertising and were willing to invest incrementally in driving their own performance. Retail media also created a more resilient revenue model, reducing the marketplace's dependence on transaction volumes.

Strategic Implications

These results validate the transformation approach: by focusing on strategic seller recruitment, data quality, and multi-faceted monetization, the marketplace achieved growth that far exceeded industry benchmarks. The 120% MoM growth and 15% revenue uplift reflect not just increased transaction volume but fundamental improvement in marketplace value proposition.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a catalogue-to-marketplace transformation typically take?

The transformation timeline varies significantly based on organizational readiness and platform choice. Platform-based transformations (using solutions like Marketplacer) typically achieve marketplace launch within 6-9 months, though the seller acquisition phase that follows extends another 6-12 months. Custom builds can require 2-3 years. Sengupta's experience at Lasoo reflects the accelerated timeline possible with platform adoption and dedicated cross-functional effort.

What's the minimum number of sellers required to launch a marketplace successfully?

There's no magic number, but quality matters more than quantity. A marketplace launched with 5-10 high-quality, credible sellers offering broad assortment can succeed if they represent genuine categories and are operationally mature. Lasoo's focus on 60+ large retailers within six months prioritized quality and credibility over rapid seller acquisition. The strategic question is whether your initial seller cohort commands customer trust and offers compelling assortment depth.

Should our marketplace model include marketplace fulfillment, or should sellers manage their own fulfillment?

This depends on your seller base and category. For B2B and bulk commerce, seller-managed fulfillment is typical—sellers operate warehouses and fulfillment infrastructure. For B2C marketplaces and lower-SKU-density categories, you may need to offer fulfillment options. Many successful marketplaces operate hybrid models: they enable seller-managed fulfillment for sophisticated sellers but provide marketplace-operated fulfillment for smaller sellers. This flexibility expands addressable seller base while managing operational complexity.

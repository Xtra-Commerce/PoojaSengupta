How to reduce delivery time by 35%, manage perishable goods across 800+ storefronts, and build sustainable delivery partnerships with platforms like UberEATS, Deliveroo, and Airtasker.

The Last-Mile Challenge in Grocery Ecommerce

Grocery ecommerce is operationally distinct from other ecommerce categories in ways that fundamentally reshape last-mile delivery strategy. While delivering a smartphone, book, or piece of furniture involves logistics complexity around routing, package handling, and timing, grocery delivery adds layers of constraint that transform the problem entirely: cold chain management, perishability windows, quality expectations, and thin unit economics that make delivery costs existential to profitability.

During Sengupta's tenure as Head of Online Merchandising & Strategic Partnerships at Coles, overseeing a $1.1B P&L and managing 800+ digital storefronts, the delivery problem was neither abstract nor theoretical. Coles' online grocery business served a customer base with very specific expectations: groceries delivered within 2-3 hour time windows, at consistent temperature, in perfect condition. Customers expected the lettuce to be crisp, the ice cream frozen, the eggs unbroken. Any deviation—a melted yogurt, a crushed banana, a late delivery—triggered customer dissatisfaction, negative reviews, and potential attrition to competitors.

The cold chain constraint is often underestimated in discussions of grocery delivery optimization. When delivering electronics or apparel, the product remains viable across temperature and humidity ranges spanning tens of degrees. Grocery products exist in narrow bands. Frozen items must remain below -18°C. Chilled products must stay between 0-5°C. Ambient temperature items can tolerate wider ranges but still face limits from seasonality and storage constraints. Add to this the perishability dimension: a product delivered in perfect condition but two days after the purchase loses value. Customers expect same-day or next-day delivery, not the 3-5 day standard in many retail categories.

This combination creates a unique operational challenge: deliver fresh, chilled, and frozen items within narrow time windows, maintaining cold chain integrity, without damaging the product, while achieving delivery economics sustainable enough to protect margin. For a $1.1B grocery business, the margin difference between a 30-minute and 60-minute delivery time window can mean tens of millions of dollars annually.

Multiply these constraints across 800 digital storefronts, each serving distinct geographies, customer concentrations, and partner availability, and the scale of the challenge becomes evident. A solution that works in dense urban areas (Sydney, Melbourne) breaks down in regional areas where partner density is lower. A partner with exceptional performance on fresh produce may struggle with chilled dairy. A system optimized for peak-hour demand may collapse during off-peak periods when partner availability drops.

Key Takeaway: Grocery delivery is not logistics—it is a system integrating cold chain management, perishability constraints, customer expectations, geographic variability, and partner economics simultaneously. Optimization requires addressing all dimensions; partial solutions create bottlenecks.

Why Partnerships Over In-House Delivery: Strategic Rationale

A common instinct for large retailers moving into ecommerce is to vertically integrate delivery: build in-house fleets, hire and train delivery personnel, invest in vehicles and cold chain infrastructure. Amazon has pursued this with Amazon Logistics. Walmart has invested in its own last-mile network. Traditional retailers see delivery as a core competency they should control.

Sengupta's strategic approach at Coles diverged from this conventional wisdom, prioritizing partnerships with specialized platforms—UberEATS, Deliveroo, Airtasker—over in-house development. This decision reflected five critical factors:

1. Capital Efficiency and Flexibility

Building a grocery delivery fleet requires substantial capex: vehicles (refrigerated vans cost $40,000-$80,000 each), cold chain equipment, warehousing for inventory buffering, and distribution center infrastructure. These assets have fixed costs regardless of demand fluctuation. Partnered delivery platforms provide variable cost structure: you pay per delivery, not per vehicle capacity. During demand fluctuations (peak season vs. low season, geography expansion vs. contraction), variable costs scale naturally without stranding capital.

For a business managing 800+ storefronts across varying geographies, this flexibility is essential. A new storefront can launch delivery service on day one by leveraging existing partner networks; building in-house capability would require months of hiring and infrastructure development.

2. Geographic Coverage Without Geographic Constraints

UberEATS, Deliveroo, and Airtasker maintain existing driver networks across hundreds of suburbs. A grocery operator can access this existing infrastructure without recruiting and training drivers from scratch. This is particularly critical in areas where building an in-house fleet is economically infeasible: regional and peri-urban areas where delivery density doesn't justify dedicated assets.

Additionally, these platforms manage their own driver economics, insurance, compliance, and labor relationships—areas where a grocery retailer has no comparative advantage. Coles benefits from partners' ongoing investment in driver apps, routing algorithms, and incentive structures rather than building these systems from scratch.

3. Technology and Operational Infrastructure

Platforms like UberEATS and Deliveroo have invested billions in real-time routing, driver coordination, customer communication, and operational analytics. These are core competencies for a logistics platform, but distraction from core business for a grocery retailer. By partnering, Coles accessed infrastructure that Coles could not have built faster or more efficiently in-house, allowing Coles' teams to focus on merchandising, category management, and customer experience—areas where Coles has genuine competitive advantage.

4. Risk Distribution and Redundancy

Relying on a single in-house fleet creates concentration risk. A labor dispute, vehicle fleet issue, or system outage affects all customers simultaneously. Partnering with multiple platforms (Coles uses UberEATS, Deliveroo, and Airtasker simultaneously) creates redundancy. If one platform experiences issues, alternative partners maintain customer service. This redundancy approach also creates competitive pressure: partners competing for Coles' volume have incentive to maintain quality and reliability.

5. Partnership as Leverage

As Coles' online grocery business scaled and demonstrated consistent volume, partnerships with delivery platforms shifted from tactical to strategic. Coles' volume provided platform partners with meaningful commercial opportunity. This leverage enabled Sengupta's team to negotiate favorable economics, develop customized solutions (e.g., specialized handling for fragile items), and integrate delivery operations with Coles' inventory and merchandising systems in ways that would be difficult with a smaller partner.

The partnership approach also creates alignment: platforms succeed when Coles succeeds, when customer satisfaction is high, and when delivery reliability exceeds customer expectations.

Key Takeaway: For most grocery operators, partnerships with specialized delivery platforms provide superior economics, geographic coverage, technology access, and risk distribution compared to in-house development. Vertical integration is only justified at extreme scale where dedicated assets achieve superior utilization and cost.

Selecting and Managing Delivery Partners: Framework and SLAs

Selecting delivery partners is not a binary decision but a portfolio optimization problem. Different partners have distinct strengths, geographies, economics, and customer bases. Successful delivery operations require a balanced partner mix aligned to specific geographic and category segments.

Partner Evaluation Framework

When Sengupta's team evaluated UberEATS, Deliveroo, and Airtasker, the assessment covered eight dimensions:

  • Geographic Coverage: Which suburbs and postal codes does each platform serve? What is the density and consistency of coverage? How frequently does coverage change?
  • Driver Availability and Reliability: What percentage of delivery requests get fulfilled? What are average response times? Are drivers trained for food/cold chain handling?
  • Cold Chain Capability: Does the platform require special equipment for frozen and chilled items? What is the infrastructure for temperature maintenance?
  • Delivery Time Windows: What time window guarantees can partners offer (e.g., 30-minute, 60-minute, 2-hour windows)?
  • Economics and Pricing: What is the per-delivery commission? Are there volume discounts? How do pricing models compare across platforms?
  • Integration and Visibility: Can the partner's system integrate with Coles' order management, inventory, and customer communication systems? What real-time tracking data is available?
  • Customer Experience and Brand Fit: What are customer reviews of the platform? Does the partner's brand alignment match Coles' positioning? How are customer complaints handled?
  • Strategic Alignment and Relationship Maturity: Is the partner interested in long-term strategic partnership or transactional delivery? Can the partner evolve capabilities as Coles' business needs change?

Service Level Agreement (SLA) Framework

Partnering with external platforms creates the need for clear, measurable SLAs that define expectations and accountability. For Coles' grocery delivery partnerships, SLA frameworks addressed:

  • Delivery Speed: Target delivery time from order placement (e.g., 95% of orders delivered within 60 minutes). Different time windows apply to different order types: orders during peak hours (6-9 PM) may have 90-minute windows, while off-peak orders may have 60-minute commitments.
  • Cold Chain Maintenance: Temperature requirements by product category (frozen: below -18°C, chilled: 0-5°C, ambient: 15-25°C). Measurement through temperature loggers on sample shipments validates compliance.
  • Product Condition: Acceptance criteria for products at delivery: eggs unbroken, produce not crushed, glass bottles intact. Measurement through post-delivery customer feedback and random audits of delivered items.
  • Order Accuracy: All requested items present and correct at delivery. Target: 99% accuracy. Measurement through customer feedback and mystery shopper audits.
  • Communication and Tracking: Real-time order status updates, driver contact information, and live GPS tracking available to customers. Measurement through system uptime and data transmission latency.
  • Exception Handling: Response protocols for customer complaints, product damage, delivery failures, or driver misconduct. Target resolution time and escalation path clearly defined.
  • Performance Incentives and Penalties: Financial incentives for exceeding SLAs (e.g., bonus for 99%+ on-time delivery), financial penalties for missing targets (e.g., fee rebates for late deliveries).

Managing Multiple Partners Simultaneously

Operating with three delivery partners (UberEATS, Deliveroo, Airtasker) simultaneously creates operational complexity but provides critical benefits. Each order is intelligently routed to the partner best positioned to deliver: geography, current driver availability, time window requirement, order characteristics (frozen vs. ambient), and SLA performance inform routing logic.

This approach requires investment in dispatch logic and order management systems capable of integrating multiple partner APIs. It also requires ongoing monitoring and performance measurement, comparing each partner across geographic region, order type, and time period. These insights inform continuous optimization: shifting volume to highest-performing partners, renegotiating SLAs with underperformers, and identifying service gaps where partner coverage is insufficient.

Key Takeaway: Partner selection is not a one-time decision but ongoing portfolio optimization. Clear SLAs with financial incentives/penalties align partner interests with business objectives. Integration of multiple partners provides redundancy, geographic coverage, and competitive pressure that maintains quality and cost discipline.

Reducing Delivery Time by 35%: Operational Strategies and Measurement

The 35% reduction in average delivery time—from baseline 85 minutes to target 55 minutes—was not achieved through a single initiative but through systematic optimization across six operational dimensions. Each improvement alone was modest; collectively, they transformed the delivery experience.

1. Fulfillment Center Network Optimization

Geographic proximity of fulfillment center to customer is the single largest driver of delivery time. A customer in Cremorne (Sydney inner-west) supplied from Coles' primary distribution center in Western Sydney involves 40+ minutes of transit alone; the same customer served from a fulfillment center in Marrickville involves 10-15 minutes of transit. Coles optimized its fulfillment network to position inventory closer to customer concentrations, reducing the distance delivery partners traveled.

This required investment in micro-fulfillment centers and local collection points, but the delivery time reduction justified the capex through reduced delivery costs (shorter distance = fewer minutes = lower per-delivery commission paid to partners) and improved customer satisfaction.

2. Order Preparation and Picking Efficiency

Customer-ready time (the time from order placement to ready-for-pickup) directly affects delivery time. Slow picking creates idle time for delivery drivers waiting for orders. Coles invested in scanning technology, workflow management, and picker training to accelerate order assembly. Automating cold chain preparation (grouping frozen items, using insulation efficiently) also reduced picking time and improved product quality.

Performance targets: 95% of orders picked and staged for delivery within 10 minutes of customer order placement (during peak hours) to 5 minutes (during off-peak). Achievement of these targets was measured and became part of in-store operations KPIs, creating accountability for fulfillment center teams.

3. Smart Routing and Batching

Delivery partners' routing algorithms batch multiple orders for efficient geographic routing, but additional optimization was possible through order batching on Coles' side. By intelligently timing and sequencing orders for pickup, Coles could provide delivery partners with pre-batched, pre-optimized pickup sequences that reduced waiting time and idle time.

Additionally, Coles' system provided delivery partners with real-time demand signals: if high-density order clusters are building in a suburb, Coles proactively offers pickup from a more central location rather than requiring partners to traverse to each individual store.

4. Cold Chain Efficiency and Packaging Optimization

Cold chain integrity requires insulation, but excess insulation adds weight, reduces order density per delivery vehicle, and increases handling time. Coles' packaging team optimized insulation strategies: specialized containers that maintain temperature with minimal weight, efficient ice pack placement, and category-specific packaging (separate chilled and frozen sections) that allowed efficient stacking and reduced damage.

These optimizations reduced fulfillment packaging time by 2-3 minutes per order while improving product condition at delivery—a rare case where optimization improved both speed and quality.

5. Driver Training and Incentive Alignment

Even with optimized routing and pickup, driver behavior affects delivery speed. Coles worked with delivery partners to establish driver training programs emphasizing efficient picking and delivery. Incentive structures aligned driver behavior with speed targets: bonuses for completing deliveries within time windows, penalties for excessive delays.

Additionally, training on product handling (cold chain maintenance, gentle handling for fragile items) reduced need for re-deliveries due to product damage, further improving net delivery time metrics.

6. Technology Integration and Real-Time Visibility

Integration of Coles' order management system with delivery partner APIs provided real-time visibility into order status, driver location, and estimated delivery times. This visibility enabled proactive exception management: if a delivery is trending late, Coles can notify the customer, offer rescheduling, or escalate to the delivery partner for intervention. Reducing exception handling downstream improves overall delivery efficiency.

Measurement and Continuous Improvement

The 35% time reduction was achieved through disciplined measurement and continuous iteration. Coles tracked delivery performance at multiple levels: by store, by delivery partner, by order type (fresh produce vs. ambient goods), by geographic region, and by time of day. These granular metrics identified specific bottlenecks and enabled targeted optimization.

For example, analysis revealed that frozen product orders had disproportionately longer delivery times because of complex cold chain staging. Specialized training and packaging protocols for frozen products reduced frozen order delivery time by 15-20%, disproportionately improving overall metrics.

Similarly, geographic analysis revealed that certain outer suburbs had consistently high delivery times due to low density. Coles adjusted its fulfillment strategy for these areas, creating local pickup points and batch delivery schedules that reduced customer expectation misalignment.

Key Takeaway: A 35% delivery time reduction is not achieved through single innovation but systematic optimization across network design, fulfillment operations, partner routing, packaging, driver incentives, and technology integration. Measurement at granular levels identifies specific bottlenecks and enables targeted improvement.

Digital Shelf and Delivery Integration: Managing 800+ Storefronts

A distinctive challenge of managing a $1.1B online grocery business across 800+ storefronts is maintaining consistency in merchandising, pricing, and availability while respecting local autonomy and partner constraints. This requires deep integration between merchandising systems and delivery operations—a connection often overlooked in typical ecommerce discussions.

The Merchandising-Delivery Interdependence

Successful grocery ecommerce requires alignment between what is merchandised and what can be delivered. If a product is featured prominently but cannot be delivered within the target time window (due to geography, supplier constraints, or partner capacity), customer satisfaction suffers. Conversely, if merchandising is overly conservative and excludes products that could deliver successfully, sales and market share suffer.

For Coles, this required building systems that provided real-time visibility into: product availability by location, supplier lead times, delivery partner capacity and geography, customer order history and preferences, and merchandising performance metrics. These systems fed into centralized assortment management that determined what products appeared on the digital shelf, at what price, with what promotional intensity, and in what geographic areas.

Assortment Management Across Geographic Variation

A grocery retailer's physical stores maintain core assortment consistency (broadly similar product selection across locations) while allowing local variation (different produce based on seasonal availability, local preferences, competitor presence). Online assortment management must accommodate similar dynamics at scale.

Coles' centralized assortment team maintained a master product list (20,000+ SKUs) that forms the basis of all digital storefronts. However, this master assortment is dynamically modified for each storefront based on:

  • Geographic Supplier Availability: Products may have different supply chains based on location (produce sourced locally vs. regionally affects availability window)
  • Delivery Partner Capability: If a particular delivery partner in a geography specializes in ambient goods and lacks frozen capability, certain frozen products are de-prioritized or bundled with orders from higher-capability partners
  • Inventory Positioning: Central assortment identifies which products should be pre-positioned in micro-fulfillment centers vs. fulfilled from regional distribution, affecting delivery-time feasibility
  • Demand Elasticity and Pricing: Local market conditions may warrant different promotional intensity for the same product

This dynamic assortment required sophisticated back-office systems integrating POS, inventory management, procurement, merchandising, and logistics. Without such integration, assortment decisions made in isolation from operational constraints would create persistent delivery failures.

Inventory Positioning and Safety Stock

For same-day and next-day delivery to be reliable, inventory must be pre-positioned in locations from which delivery partners can serve customers quickly. This required investment in regional and micro-fulfillment centers distributed across geographies. However, this distribution increases total inventory carrying costs and obsolescence risk for perishable products.

Coles optimized this through analytics integrating demand forecasting, product shelf life, supplier lead times, and logistics geometry. Products with long shelf life and consistent demand (ambient pantry items) could be pre-positioned in regional facilities; perishable or seasonal items (fresh produce, dairy) required just-in-time sourcing and fulfillment from primary distribution centers even if it extended delivery time slightly.

Post-Delivery Feedback Loop

Customer feedback on delivery experience (product condition, delivery timing, packaging quality) fed directly into merchandise and logistics decisions. High damage rates on certain products triggered packaging redesign or category team review. Late deliveries in certain geographies triggered supply chain or partner adjustments. Product freshness complaints triggered changes to inventory positioning or sourcing.

This feedback loop created continuous learning: each delivery became data point informing future assortment, sourcing, and logistics decisions.

Key Takeaway: Managing 800+ digital storefronts requires integration between merchandising and logistics that is unusual in traditional retail. Real-time visibility into product availability, supplier capability, delivery partner constraints, and demand enables dynamic assortment and inventory positioning that balances growth with operational feasibility.

Scaling Delivery Operations: From Pilot to Enterprise Scale at $1.1B

Scaling last-mile delivery from pilot to enterprise operations requires discipline across four dimensions: strategic partnership development, operational standardization, measurement and continuous improvement, and organizational capability building.

Phase 1: Pilot and Learning (Months 1-6)

Sengupta's approach began with focused pilots in 2-3 geographies where delivery partner presence was strong and delivery density was highest. These pilots tested partnership models, delivery operations, customer experience, and financial economics before scaling. Success criteria were clear: on-time delivery rate exceeding 95%, customer satisfaction scores exceeding 4.5/5.0, and delivery cost per order within acceptable margin.

Pilot geographies were deliberately chosen to be adjacent to existing physical store locations with strong ecommerce customer bases, allowing cross-learning between online and offline operations.

Phase 2: Geographic Expansion (Months 6-18)

Success in pilot geographies enabled expansion to adjacent suburbs and regions. Rather than nation-wide rollout, expansion was sequential, allowing each new geography to benefit from learnings from prior locations. New geographic entries focused on areas with strong delivery partner presence, high customer density, and clear business case.

Expansion also involved relationship development with delivery partners: as Coles' volume grew, relationships evolved from tactical to strategic. UberEATS, Deliveroo, and Airtasker invested in dedicated account management, customized integration, and service level agreements reflecting growing partnership maturity.

Phase 3: Operationalization and Standardization (Months 18-36)

As delivery scaled across hundreds of locations, Sengupta's team standardized operational processes: picking procedures, packaging standards, quality control checkpoints, driver training, customer communication templates. This standardization reduced variance and enabled performance management at scale.

Technology investments accelerated: order management system integration with delivery APIs, real-time inventory visibility, analytics dashboards tracking performance by location and partner, and feedback loops integrating customer experience into operational decision-making.

Phase 4: Performance Optimization and Next-Layer Capability (Year 2+)

With delivery operations stabilized at scale, optimization efforts focused on unit economics and customer experience. The 35% delivery time reduction, packaging optimization, fulfillment center network refinement, and driver training programs emerged from systematic performance analysis and continuous improvement discipline.

Additionally, organizational capability evolved: hiring of dedicated logistics professionals, development of relationships with delivery platform leadership teams, and creation of feedback mechanisms linking online delivery performance to physical store operations.

Key Scaling Challenges and Solutions

Challenge 1: Inconsistency Across Locations — Solution: Centralized governance with distributed execution. Master processes and standards defined centrally, but execution adapted to local conditions (store layout, team capabilities, partner availability). Regular audits and mystery shopping verified consistency.

Challenge 2: Partner Capacity Constraints — Solution: Multi-partner strategy. When one partner hits capacity constraints, load is shifted to alternatives. Proactive capacity planning and demand signaling to partners prevented delivery failures during peak demand.

Challenge 3: Changing Partner Economics — Solution: Relationship development and volume leverage. As Coles' volume grew, the negotiation balance shifted. Regular SLA reviews and renegotiations maintained favorable economics while rewarding partners for consistent performance.

Challenge 4: Organizational Coordination — Solution: Cross-functional governance. Monthly steering committees aligned merchandising, supply chain, logistics, and customer service teams around delivery performance metrics and strategic direction.

Key Takeaway: Scaling delivery to enterprise level requires phase-gate progression from pilot to expansion to standardization to optimization. Technology, process standardization, partnership development, and organizational alignment must advance in parallel; gaps in any dimension create bottlenecks.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is last-mile delivery uniquely challenging in grocery ecommerce compared to other categories?

Grocery delivery involves multiple constraints that don't apply to hard goods: perishable products require temperature-controlled handling and delivery within narrow time windows; margins are thin, making delivery cost economics critical; and customer expectations for freshness and quality are high. Sengupta's experience at Coles demonstrated that solving for grocery delivery requires integrated solutions that address cold chain management, route optimization, partnership economics, and merchandising integration simultaneously. Hard goods delivery can often tolerate longer time windows and less precise handling; grocery cannot.

How should a marketplace operator choose between in-house delivery and third-party partnerships?

The decision depends on scale, geography, and capital constraints. In-house delivery makes economic sense at extremely high volume (500+ daily orders in concentrated geography) where asset utilization justifies capex and operational overhead. For most operators, especially those managing geographically distributed locations, partnering with specialized platforms (UberEATS, Deliveroo, Airtasker) is superior. Partnerships provide capital efficiency, geographic coverage, technology infrastructure, driver recruitment, and insurance without requiring vertical integration. Sengupta's approach at Coles combined multiple partners simultaneously to ensure redundancy, geographic coverage, and competitive cost pressure.

How can operators maintain merchandising quality and consistency across 800+ digital storefronts served by different delivery partners?

Quality consistency requires centralized governance with distributed execution. Maintain single master assortment and pricing across all storefronts, synchronized in real-time. Provide each delivery partner with clear fulfillment SLAs for pick-to-delivery time, temperature maintenance, and condition standards. Implement post-delivery customer feedback integration, linking delivery quality metrics back to central merchandising teams. Use data analytics to identify patterns (e.g., certain products have higher damage rates with certain partners) and adjust assortment or logistics accordingly. Sengupta's work at Coles managed consistency through integrated POS, inventory, and logistics systems treating all 800+ locations as distributed nodes of a single enterprise.

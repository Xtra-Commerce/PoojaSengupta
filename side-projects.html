Side Projects

I build outside structured platforms as often as I build within them. Some of these projects became businesses. Some remained experiments. Some failed. All of them were built deliberately.

Evryfit and The Hoodel

Evryfit was a fitness-focused direct-to-consumer ecommerce brand. The Hoodel was a product I created from scratch, designed by me and taken through sourcing and manufacturing. This was not a white-label operation. It required direct product development, supply chain setup, and end-to-end execution from concept to market.

The work involved identifying a gap in the fitness product space, designing a physical product, managing the manufacturing process, and building the ecommerce infrastructure to sell it. It was an exercise in product development discipline applied outside the software world.

Bathroom Planner Tool

Built while helping manage a family home construction project. Existing tools for tracking and selecting bathroom fittings and tiles were not practical for how the decisions actually needed to be made. I built a functional planner tool to solve the problem directly. It was a small, focused build driven by a real operational constraint.

Vintage Designer Resale

An attempt to build a resale model around vintage designer jewelry, including pieces from Chanel, Hermes, and YSL. The concept was sound, authenticated vintage luxury at accessible price points, sold through a curated online channel.

It did not scale as expected. The structural misalignment between supply availability and consistent buyer demand made the model difficult to sustain. I treated it as a learning experiment and moved on.

Not everything I build becomes a business. But the instinct to build, test, and learn from the result is consistent across all of it.
